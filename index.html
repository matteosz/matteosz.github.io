Predicting poverty in Africa through time using publicly available data

    Made with:

Understanding the distribution of poverty is essential to planning humanitarian interventions and managing the allocation of limited resources. Predictive mapping from remotely sensed data is a promising approach for creating objective estimates of poverty. In this project, I worked in a team of 3 people to optimize and extend the geographic scope of a promising architecture to predict poverty through time from multimodal, remotely sensed data such as satellite images. We included three new African countries (Mali, Uganda and Niger) and compared them to previously reported results for Ethiopia, Nigeria, Tanzania and Malawi. Thereafter, we optimized the previous CNN architecture, by adopting a more complex ResNet152, which resulted in significant performance gains.

GLAD: Gossip Learning Averaging Distance

    Made with:

Abstract: Interest in Federated Learning (FL) has recently increased significantly due to the looming need for privacy among companies and institutions coupled with the rapid development of powerful devices that make Edge Computing advantageous. Current research on FL is primarily focused on privacy, resilience, and improving performance. Researchers often consider only a single metric, leaving open issues concerning the integration between the diversity in data quality, resource capabilities, and network topologies, which characterize highly heterogeneous networks. In this paper, we analyze Federated Learning by presenting use cases and applications, the metrics involved, and the variety of architectures. We propose GLAD (Gossip Learning Averaging Distance), an entirely decentralized and synchronous system based on the gossip protocol that introduces metrics, such as resource capability and data quality, in the aggregation of several models. By computing a score for each node based on these metrics, we present a weighted averaging process that is effectively executed only when the Euclidean distance among the weights of neurons within an artificial neural network model with the same coordinates between different nodes is under a specified threshold. This approach enables better performance in terms of global accuracy, especially for heterogeneous networks, compared to other traditional FL algorithms that take advantage of nodes with better resources and data quality over poorer nodes during the merging step. Furthermore, we discuss how performance changes with respect to the network topology and variations in the analyzed metrics.
